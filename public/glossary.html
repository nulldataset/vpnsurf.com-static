VPN & security glossary

Short definitions for terms we use on VPNsurf.com and across the site.

Encryption
The process of scrambling data so only authorized parties can read it. VPNs use encryption to protect your internet traffic so that anyone intercepting it (e.g. on Wi‑Fi) sees unreadable data instead of your actual browsing.
IP address
A unique identifier assigned to your device on a network (e.g. the internet). Websites and services can use it to infer your location and sometimes identity. A VPN assigns you a different IP from its server, so your real IP is hidden.
Kill switch
A VPN feature that blocks all internet traffic if the VPN connection drops. That prevents your real IP and traffic from being exposed until the VPN reconnects. Especially useful on unstable or public networks.
No-logs policy
A commitment by a VPN provider not to collect or store records of your online activity (sites visited, connection times, etc.). Audited no-logs policies are a strong privacy indicator when choosing a VPN.
VPN (Virtual Private Network)
A service that creates an encrypted tunnel between your device and a remote server. Your traffic goes through that server, so your IP appears to be the server’s and your data is protected from eavesdroppers.
Tunneling
The method of wrapping your data (e.g. in an encrypted “tunnel”) so it can be sent securely through another network. VPNs use tunneling protocols (e.g. WireGuard, OpenVPN) to carry your traffic to the VPN server.
DNS (Domain Name System)
The system that turns domain names (e.g. example.com) into IP addresses. If your DNS requests go to your ISP or a third party, they can see which sites you look up. A VPN can route DNS through its own servers to avoid that.
HTTPS
A secure version of HTTP that encrypts data between your browser and the website. Always prefer HTTPS (look for the lock icon) when entering passwords or payment details. A VPN adds another layer by encrypting the whole connection.
Leak
When your real IP, DNS requests, or other identifying data are exposed despite using a VPN. Good VPNs include leak protection and allow you to verify that no leaks occur (e.g. via DNS or WebRTC tests).
Two-factor authentication (2FA)
A security method that requires a second proof of identity (e.g. a code from an app or SMS) in addition to your password. Enabling 2FA on your VPN and important accounts greatly reduces the risk of unauthorized access.
